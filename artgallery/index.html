Group Exhibition curated by Tammy Tran

ABOUT

This art exhibition is a way to enable artists to create a message to share to their audience using their preferred form of art. I had wanted to create this exhibition as it feels relevant to the current world and how while people online may feel strongly about a topic – they end up silent and unable to express how they truly feel about issues that are affecting our world. I wish to inspire the viewer to feel like they have the stepping stones to be able to speak out about what they believe is right in the world. After all, it may seem scary out there with the current ongoing events but it is important to acknowledge that we aren’t alone and that if we work together as a group effort, things can be done. Even if it is small steps, isn’t it better then nothing at all? That is why I chose the message “Make a Statement” as the title for this exhibition. While I could’ve focused on a specific topic, it only feels like a small slice of what artists out there are working towards standing up against. Even despite the controversies they may face, they still made a statement that leaves an impression on the viewer as well as myself.

In the selection of my artists, I opted for a focus on a variety of different topics that are being spoken about. From the critique on how the working class are treated to a more self-aware statement on how art galleries are elitist, I wish to make a general space that you can speak out about any topic that may seem troubling to the viewer. Art is usually associated as a way to express yourself and your thoughts, which is shown to its greatest extent with the artists and the artworks that were chosen to be displayed in this piece.

Much of my selection started with the artworks themselves and the themes of what they are critiquing. However, there are a selection of artists that I had picked out from media I had personally consumed such as social media statements about the artwork in question or a documentary I had watched for another class.

Marcel Duchamp
I had learned about Duchamp in my previous semester of college education, being able to find the courage to publish a urinal to another art exhibition. While his original intention may not have been to critique how art galleries often are elitist in how they perceive what may be “low effort art” as something much more valuable in comparison to other artists, who have poured their time and effort into their works to only not get much appreciation in comparison. Even if the original exhibition that it was presented in did not show it in the show area, it still left a mark in art history and fits perfectly with the theme that this curatorial group exhibition is going for.

Norman Rockwell
This artist was one of the few artists I had picked out for the artwork instead of the artist themselves. I am very interested in this artist, however, as I never realized that I have looked at the artworks created by this particular artist until looking at other works that he has made. Seeing how Rockwell uses his artworks to speak about the culture and the events ongoing in the world, it definitely left a statement to have an artist like Rockwell creating something that shines a light on how this child was being treated. Even if the child themselves had never met Rockwell, the fact that she felt admiration for what he had done for her is plenty enough of a statement to work in line with this exhibition’s theme.

Banksy
While this artist has not revealed his identity, the message that his art leaves is still very much present. I vividly remember hearing about Banksy in the past, especially with the artwork I had chosen to exhibit in this exhibition. Even despite the attempts to scrub off the work that Banksy has done, the statement that his artwork had left imprinted on the wall and left an even more glaring message. Much of his work is rooted in making a statement, being a political activist and all.

Yoko Ono
I remember hearing about the performance art that she had done a very long time ago on social media. Even years later, her performance had left a mark on me as an AFAB. That is the sort of impression that I would like to leave behind on my viewers with this exhibition as a whole, so even if her art isn’t the conventional artworks that could be hung up or displayed on a pedestal, I deeply wanted to include her as a part of my exhibition.

Tracey Emin
Emin’s artworks stood out to me when I first saw the artwork I was originally going to go with: “My Bed” (1998) As it felt like it definitely made a statement on the human condition and shines a light on how people aren’t perfect, especially after a difficult time such as a breakup. Emin’s artworks came off as making a statement immediately, but it was only when I had seen the artwork I had chosen to display here that I had figured out what exactly was the message that I wanted her to present in my group exhibition.

Jens Haaning
The message that Jens Haaning’s most famous artwork left behind intrigued me a lot. I knew that I wanted an artist and artwork that worked to critique the treatment of the working class and the wealth gap, and I believe Haaning’s “artwork” had certainly left that statement with the legal action of the gallery that exhibited the framed artworks towards Haaning.

Ai Weiwei
I was immediately drawn by this artist when being introduced to him in the documentary about propaganda I was watching. Even despite getting backlash from the Chinese government and being detained, he still persevered with what he believed in. I want to share that type of message with the viewers of the exhibition.

Lori Larusso
Larusso’s artworks caught my attention with its artstyle, being a clean yet visually distinct of the objects that are featured within the artwork. It reminds me of corporate art styles of the 2000’s, which does fit with the idea of her art representing awareness towards the overconsumption of consumers and how it affects the things around you like other animals.
